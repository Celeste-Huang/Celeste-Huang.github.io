Elevated water table depth under 8-year warming turns carbon sink to source in permafrost
Xin Huang1,2, Xingjie Lu3, Brian Izbicki1,4, Elaine Pegoraro5, Lifen Jiang1, Ted Schuur1,4 Yiqi Luo1,2,4
1 Center for Ecosystem Science and Society, Northern Arizona University, Flagstaff, AZ, USA
2 School of informatics, Computing, and Cyber Systems, Northern Arizona University, Flagstaff, AZ, USA
3 School of Atmospheric Sciences, Sun Yat-Sen University, Guangzhou, China
4 Department of Biological Sciences, Northern Arizona University, Flagstaff, AZ, USA
5 Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, Berkeley, CA, USA

Permafrost soils contain enormous amount of carbon (C) that is vulnerable to be released into atmosphere under a warming climate. The magnitude of permafrost C release is regulated by both biotic and abiotic changes (e.g., changes in thaw depth (TD) and water table depth (WTD)) after permafrost thaws. The abiotic changes control multiple ecological processes and influence the net carbon balance of permafrost ecosystems. The abiotic changes can lead to a shift between C source or sink of permafrost, depending on their relative impacts on ecosystem productivity and microbial decomposition. However, how changes in TD and WTD control the net carbon balance have not been well examined. In this study, we combined 8-year observations from a soil warming experiment in Alaska with a terrestrial ecosystem model using a data assimilation technique. The calibrated model is used to simulate C cycle in permafrost in response to changes in TD and WTD and the underlying mechanisms. Our results showed that net ecosystem exchange (NEE) is more sensitive to changes in WTD than those in TD, implying that hydrological dynamics have an significant impact on C cycle after permafrost thaws. Both responses of gross primary productivity (GPP) and ecosystem respiration (Reco) to WTD have a changing point where flooding curbs plant photosynthesis and microbial activities. However, the suppression of photosynthesis is more than that in microbial respiration. It suggests the stronger acclimation and adaptation of microbe community in response to warming.
